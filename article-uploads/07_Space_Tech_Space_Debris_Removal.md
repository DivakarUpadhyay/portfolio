# The Rise of Space "Scavengers": The Trillion-Dollar New Frontier of Orbital Environmental Services

## Ghost Ships of the New Age of Navigation

Human exploration history has always progressed in an interweaving of greatness and chaos. During the Age of Exploration in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, the fleets of Columbus and Magellan parted the mists of unknown seas, not only discovering new continents but connecting isolated civilizations in unprecedented ways, opening the prelude to global trade. However, the B-side of history is that accompanying this prosperous route were countless ships that sank due to storms, pirates, wars, or simple accidents. These "ghost ships" laden with cargo and hope, along with their wreckage, became unpredictable deadly obstacles on the new shipping lanes in the following centuries, threatening the safety of successors and the stability of commercial routes.

Today, we are in the midst of an entirely new age of navigation—interstellar exploration. Earth orbit is the "new ocean" of our era. Over the past sixty-plus years, we have launched thousands of satellites, probes, and space stations into this vast territory. Like the Spanish galleons of expeditions past, they have brought revolutionary benefits to human society, including global real-time communications, precise GPS navigation, and climate change monitoring. But we have also left behind a mess. Defunct satellites, discarded rocket stages, and countless fragments from accidental explosions now float in orbit like ghost ships, forming an increasingly crowded "digital reef zone."

According to estimates by the European Space Agency (ESA), more than one million debris objects larger than one centimeter are currently orbiting Earth at the astonishing speed of up to 8 kilometers per second, and any collision with an active satellite could be catastrophic. They are the digital shipwrecks left by our spirit of exploration, increasingly threatening our shared route to the sea of stars. Therefore, cleaning up these orbital "shipwrecks" is not only about clearing obstacles but also about ensuring that this expedition of the new age of navigation can continue. We need a new generation of "orbital guardians" whose mission is not to discover new worlds but to protect our shared territory leading to the future. This is not only an urgent responsibility but also the beginning of an unprecedented enormous business opportunity.

## From "Gazing at the Stars" to "Managing Space": A Paradigm Shift

For a long time, our attitude toward space has been that of passive "users." We viewed orbit as an infinitely wide, inexhaustible shelf on which we could place our assets at will, rarely considering its limited carrying capacity and the fragility of the environment. This linear "Launch and Leave" thinking is pushing us toward the edge of a disaster called "Kessler Syndrome."

This theory, proposed by NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, describes a disturbing future: when the density of objects in low Earth orbit reaches a critical point, debris from one collision will trigger a chain reaction of more collisions, eventually forming an impenetrable debris belt that completely locks humanity's access to space. This once sounded like a plot from the science fiction movie "Gravity," but with the deployment of mega satellite constellation plans like SpaceX's Starlink and OneWeb, orbital density is increasing at an unprecedented rate, and the risk of Kessler Syndrome has moved from theory to the edge of reality.

We stand at a critical turning point. We must complete a profound paradigm shift: from an era of passively "using" space to an era of actively "managing" the space environment. Space is no longer merely a location for infrastructure; it itself is critical infrastructure that requires active management, maintenance, and investment. This means we need to establish a sustainable "Circular Space Economy." In this new paradigm, satellite in-orbit servicing, life extension, refueling, and responsible End-of-Life (EoL) handling will no longer be optional but will become standard operating procedures for all space activities.

## Tools for Pioneering the New Frontier: Why Now?

Viewing space as a frontier to be managed—the realization of this vision is no accident but the result of a series of technological, market, and economic factors working together. These "Enablers" together have created a historic window, turning orbital environmental services from a distant dream into a viable commercial proposition.

**Technology Drivers:**

First is the maturation of **intelligent robotics and artificial intelligence**. In the past, capturing a "non-cooperative target" (such as out-of-control debris) spinning at high speed without any cooperative measures was considered one of the most difficult challenges in space operations. But now, advanced robotic technology—such as precise robotic arms, flexible capture nets, and reliable harpoon systems—combined with AI-driven visual recognition and autonomous navigation (RPO - Rendezvous and Proximity Operations), makes rendezvous and capture of these "space mustangs" possible. Ukrainian-background startup **Kurs Orbital** is working on developing standardized autonomous rendezvous and docking systems, with the goal of providing "plug-and-play" solutions for in-orbit servicing, greatly reducing mission complexity.

Second, breakthroughs in **new propulsion technologies** provide more cost-effective options for debris deorbiting. For example, the laser ablation technology being explored by companies like **Orbital Lasers** can precisely "blow" debris toward the atmosphere for burnup by illuminating debris surfaces with ground-based or space-based lasers, causing localized vaporization that generates tiny thrust—all without physical contact. This non-contact method avoids secondary debris that could be generated during capture, providing a potentially efficient approach for cleaning up large quantities of small debris.

Finally, the leap in **Space Situational Awareness (SSA)** capabilities has provided the necessary "eyes" and "brain" for orbital cleanup. Global monitoring systems composed of ground-based radar, optical telescope networks, and AI algorithms can now detect and track far smaller debris than before and precisely predict their trajectories. Companies like **Spaceflux** and **LeoLabs** are building such global sensor networks, providing real-time collision warnings for satellite operators, as well as identifying high-risk targets for future cleanup missions, enabling cleanup work to be "targeted."

**Market and Economic Drivers:**

Technology maturation is only half the story; an even more powerful catalyst comes from the market. The commercial space revolution led by SpaceX has reduced the cost of accessing space by more than an order of magnitude. When launch costs per kilogram dropped from tens of thousands of dollars in the past to thousands of dollars today, many business models previously shelved for economic infeasibility, such as in-orbit servicing, suddenly became within reach.

At the same time, the rise of mega satellite constellations has created unprecedented **intrinsic demand** for orbital services. When a constellation has thousands or even tens of thousands of satellites, its operators must consider how to efficiently and responsibly handle end-of-life satellites. This is no longer an optional add-on service but is key to whether their business model can be sustained. This has created a massive and predictable market for "space tow truck" End-of-Life (EoL) services.

Finally, pressure from **insurance and regulation** is becoming the "stick" driving market development. When satellite operators insure their multi-hundred-million-dollar assets, insurance companies increasingly require them to demonstrate plans for collision avoidance and handling of decommissioned satellites. At the same time, regulatory bodies like the U.S. Federal Communications Commission (FCC) have shortened satellite deorbiting timelines from 25 years to 5 years, greatly increasing demand for efficient deorbiting solutions. These external pressures are collectively forcing the market toward more responsible behavior patterns.

## Future Vision: The Birth of the Orbital Economy

As these technological and market forces converge, an entirely new economic landscape is unfolding in Earth orbit. Orbit will no longer be the final stop and graveyard for satellites but will become a vibrant commercial district. An entirely new "Orbital Economy" is forming, with potential far exceeding our imagination today.

In this future, a series of emerging industries are rising:

| Industry Category | Core Business | Representative Companies and Cases |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Active Debris Removal (ADR)** | Handling legacy, ownerless dangerous debris | **ClearSpace** (Switzerland): Won ESA contract, will execute the world's first commercial ADR mission (ClearSpace-1). |
| **End-of-Life Services (EoL)** | Providing "space tow truck" deorbiting services for satellites about to fail | **Astroscale** (Japan): Successfully demonstrated magnetic capture technology through ELSA-d mission and partners with constellation operators like OneWeb. |
| **In-Orbit Servicing/Manufacturing (ISAM)** | Repair, refueling, upgrading, assembling space assets | **Northrop Grumman** (USA): Its MEV (Mission Extension Vehicle) has successfully provided in-orbit life extension services for multiple satellites. |

According to multiple market forecasts, this emerging market has enormous growth potential. For example, **Fortune Business Insights** predicts that the space debris monitoring and removal market alone will grow from $940 million in 2022 to $1.53 billion by 2029 [1]. **MarketsandMarkets**' forecast is even more optimistic, believing the market will reach $600 million by 2028 at a compound annual growth rate of 41.7% [2]. These figures are just the tip of the iceberg. When we expand our view to include broader in-orbit services like satellite life extension and refueling, the potential value is in the hundreds of billions. This is an entirely new, infrastructure-driven massive market.

## A Call to Space Builders of the New Era

Historical experience tells us that every pioneering of a new frontier gives rise to "picks and shovels" businesses serving the pioneers. In the California Gold Rush of the 19th century, those who truly got rich were not the mud-covered gold prospectors but people like Levi Strauss who provided them with durable jeans and tools. Today, orbital environmental services are the "picks and shovels" business of the space economy—a blue ocean market full of opportunities.

**For entrepreneurs**, now is the best time to dive in. Market demand is clearly visible, but solutions are still in early stages. Opportunities exist in every segment: from developing lower-cost, more reliable modular capture tools (like standardized docking interfaces), to writing smarter mission planning software capable of handling complex orbital environments; from building more precise, wider-coverage space situational awareness data platforms, to designing specialized insurance products for in-orbit services. Don't just think about building a cooler "cleanup spacecraft"—think about how to become an indispensable infrastructure provider in this emerging ecosystem.

**For investors**, investing in orbital services is investing in the foundation of next-generation space infrastructure. This is not just about cleaning up garbage; it's about building a sustainable, scalable space economy. When evaluating projects, beyond focusing on technological originality, also examine their understanding of business models and scalability. Look for teams that not only have great visions but also have pragmatic execution capabilities. They understand that in orbit, reliability and cost-effectiveness are far more important than flashy features. Investing in companies committed to reducing in-orbit service costs, improving mission success rates, and building scalable platforms will yield rich returns over the next decade.

Like the railroads a century ago and the internet decades ago, orbital services are paving the way to unlock the full potential of the space economy. They will reduce the risks of space missions, enhance the value of space assets, and ultimately enable us to conduct larger-scale, more complex activities among the stars. To become pioneers of this era and together build prosperous routes to the sea of stars—this enterprise is perfectly timed.
