## Executive Summary

The article asserts that enterprises adopt blockchain infrastructure not from ideological commitment, but when implementation complexity is removed. Corporations need solutions to business problems—faster settlement, reduced reconciliation risk—not blockchain ownership.

---

## The Core Insight

> "Adoption happens when the chain disappears or becomes invisible."

Corporations seek operational improvements, not infrastructure control. They desire speed and transparency without managing the underlying systems.

---

## Three Adoption Pathways

| Pathway | Description | Best For |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Applications on Existing Chains** | Building apps on top of established blockchain networks | Startups, FinTech companies |
| **Partner-Deployed Solutions** | Partners deploying use cases onto protocol-owned chains | Mid-market enterprises |
| **Turnkey Infrastructure** | Protocols offering complete managed services | Large corporations |

---

## The Orchestration Principle

Protocol teams shouldn't sell blockchain directly. Instead, they should enable existing partners—integrators, custodians, processors—to deliver blockchain solutions seamlessly.

### Key Partners to Engage:

1. **Consulting Firms:** Accenture, Deloitte, McKinsey
2. **Technical Integrators:** Alchemy, Fireblocks, Chainalysis
3. **Custodians:** Coinbase Custody, BitGo, Anchorage
4. **Payment Processors:** Circle, Paxos, Ripple

---

## Strategic Recommendations

- [ ] Shift GTM focus from "convince corporates" to "enable partners"
- [ ] Engage relevant integration firms
- [ ] Provide technical enablement and simplified integration pathways
- [ ] Treat design partnerships as critical feedback mechanisms before mainnet launch

---

## Regional Context: South Korean Case Study

South Korean conglomerates initially built proprietary chains:

- **Klaytn** (Kakao)
- **Link** (LINE)
- **Wemix** (Wemade)

However, they increasingly pilot on existing infrastructure after recognizing operational challenges. This validates the core thesis: enterprises want results, not technology ownership.
