# The Next Chapter of Data Infrastructure: From Data Lake to Data Mesh

## The Hook: The Promise and Curse of Data

Over the past decade, we have witnessed a data gold rush. Enterprises have invested billions of dollars, recruited top talent, and built massive technology stacks around a grand promise: data is the new oil. The core idea was that if we could gather data scattered across every corner of the organization into a central repository—the "data lake"—we could unlock unprecedented insights, drive innovation, and ultimately dominate markets. However, for many expectant enterprises, this promise has soured. Rather than becoming clear springs of insight, data lakes have often degenerated into data swamps: chaotic quagmires filled with outdated, unreliable, and incomprehensible data. According to estimates, up to 80% of data analytics projects end in failure, and data quality issues cost the U.S. economy over $3 trillion annually. Our obsession with data centralization inadvertently created a massive bottleneck. A centralized data engineering team, no matter how talented, cannot keep up with the organization's increasingly growing, diversified, and real-time data demands. The promise of data remains valid, but the path to realizing it requires fundamental change. We are at the cusp of a paradigm shift, needing to move from an obsession with centralized control to a decentralized, edge-empowered future.

## The Paradigm Shift: From Monolithic Data Lake to Distributed Data Mesh

We believe the answer to the data swamp dilemma is not to build a bigger, deeper data lake, but to completely overturn its underlying logic. The future belongs not to monolithic data architectures but to a distributed new paradigm called the "Data Mesh." The core idea of the data mesh is to shift data ownership and responsibility from central data teams to the business domains themselves that best understand the data. This is not just a technical architecture upgrade but a profound organizational and cultural revolution. It treats data as a product (Data as a Product), owned, developed, and maintained by cross-functional teams distributed across various business domains. In this model, the sales team is responsible not only for its CRM system but also for the "customer order data product" it generates. This data product has clear definitions, reliable Service Level Agreements (SLAs), and can be easily discovered and used by other teams in the organization through standardized API interfaces. This transformation is similar to software development's evolution from monolithic applications to flexible microservices architecture. Microservices allow development teams to build and deploy services independently and quickly; similarly, the data mesh grants domain teams autonomy, enabling them to rapidly respond to business needs and deliver high-quality data products. This fundamentally solves the central data team bottleneck problem, transforming data innovation from the privilege of a few experts into a capability of the entire organization.

## The "Why Now": The Confluence of Three Driving Forces

Why is the era of data mesh arriving now? We see the confluence of three macro trends, together laying the foundation for this paradigm shift.

First is **technological maturation**. The proliferation of cloud-native technologies, especially containerization (Docker), orchestration systems (Kubernetes), and microservices architecture, has provided standardized infrastructure for building distributed systems. Over the past decade, we have learned how to manage and deploy distributed applications at scale, and now it's time to apply these experiences to the data domain. At the same time, data processing technology itself has made great strides. Stream processing platforms like Apache Kafka and Flink have made real-time data processing the norm rather than the exception. More importantly, the emergence of distributed SQL query engines like Trino (formerly PrestoSQL) allows users to perform federated queries on data distributed across different systems without moving the data. This capability is key to implementing the "self-serve data infrastructure" principle of the data mesh, breaking the traditional constraint that data must first be loaded (ETL) into a central data warehouse before it can be analyzed.

Second is **dramatic changes in market demand**. In the digital-native era, the focus of business competition has shifted from efficiency to speed and intelligence. Winners are companies that can most quickly transform data into product features, personalized experiences, and intelligent decisions. Traditional batch-processing-dominated data architectures, with delivery cycles measured in weeks or months, can no longer satisfy this thirst for real-time capability. Business teams are no longer satisfied with passively receiving reports from central data teams; they need direct, real-time access to and analysis of data to react in rapidly changing markets. This pressure is forcing enterprises to seek new models that can release data productivity at scale, and the data mesh's decentralized model perfectly meets this need.

Finally is **the evolution of organizational philosophy**. Domain-Driven Design (DDD) thinking, originating from the software development field, is beginning to permeate broader enterprise management. DDD emphasizes decomposing complex systems into highly cohesive, loosely coupled modules that correspond to business domains. This philosophy aligns perfectly with the data mesh. Enterprises are beginning to realize that delegating technical decision-making authority to teams closest to business problems can stimulate stronger autonomy and innovation. The rise of cross-functional teams has also provided the organizational foundation for implementing the data mesh. When a team includes product managers, engineers, and data analysts, having them take end-to-end responsibility for their data products becomes natural.

## Future Vision: Data as a Product and Emerging Investment Opportunities

In the new world of the data mesh, we will see the rise of an entirely new data ecosystem. At its core is the concept of "Data as a Product." Data is no longer the "exhaust" left behind after business processes end, but is carefully designed, well-documented, reliably quality-assured, and easy-to-use first-class citizens. Each data product has a clear "data product manager" who is responsible for the product's entire lifecycle—from requirements definition, development, and launch to eventual deprecation. Like software products, data products have version control, Service Level Objectives (SLOs), and provide services externally through standardized APIs (such as SQL, GraphQL, or gRPC). This will give rise to a vibrant internal data marketplace, where teams can discover, evaluate, and use data products published by other teams in a central data catalog, just like finding apps in the App Store. This model will greatly reduce the friction costs of data collaboration and accelerate the pace of innovation.

This profound transformation also brings enormous business opportunities for entrepreneurs and investors. We foresee three key investment areas:

1.  **Self-Serve Data Platform**: The success of the data mesh depends on a powerful underlying platform that allows domain teams to easily create, deploy, monitor, and manage their data products in a self-service manner. This requires a highly automated and abstracted set of tools covering data storage, computing, pipeline orchestration, authentication, and access control. We are looking for companies committed to building a "Heroku for data" or "Vercel for data products," whose goal is to provide an exceptionally smooth experience for data developers. Companies like dbt Labs have already taken important steps in this direction with their analytics engineering workflow platform, which allows analysts to easily build reliable data models using SQL.

2.  **Federated Governance & Discovery**: Decentralization does not mean chaos. The data mesh requires a "Federated Computational Governance" model that ensures data security, compliance, and interoperability across the organization while granting domain teams autonomy. This requires globally enforced policies (such as data privacy regulations) and computationally implemented policies automatically enforced by the platform. At the same time, a powerful central data catalog is essential—it is not just a list of data assets but an intelligent data discovery engine that helps users find the most relevant and trustworthy data products. Data catalog vendors like Alation and Collibra are moving in this direction, but we believe new-generation tools designed natively for data mesh will have greater opportunity.

3.  **Native Data Mesh Applications and Practices**: As the data mesh philosophy gains popularity, we will see more and more companies adopting this architecture. Early pioneers like European fashion e-commerce giant Zalando and financial software company Intuit have achieved significant business results through implementing the data mesh. Zalando can support hundreds of analytics teams innovating simultaneously, while Intuit uses the data mesh to power its TurboTax and QuickBooks products. Meanwhile, companies like Starburst, whose core product—the Trino query engine—is one of the key technologies for implementing the data mesh, enable enterprises to analyze data in place without expensive data replication and ETL. Investment opportunities lie in companies that provide not only technology but also best practices and consulting services for implementing the data mesh.

## Call to Action: To the New Generation of Data Builders

We stand at the beginning of a new decade for data infrastructure. The monolithic, centralized data lake model, once the cure for data silos, has now become a bottleneck for innovation. The decentralized, domain-oriented, data-as-product philosophy represented by the data mesh points the way forward.

**For entrepreneurs**, this is an excellent opportunity to define the market. Stop building yet another ETL tool or data visualization dashboard. Instead, think about how to provide infrastructure and tools for the three pillars of the data mesh—domain ownership, data as a product, self-serve platform, and federated governance. The greatest opportunity lies in platform companies that can significantly lower the barrier to data mesh implementation and create seamless experiences for data product developers. Focus on building tools that developers love, because in this transformation, data engineers and analysts will be the new kings.

**For investors**, the evaluation criteria need to change. We should no longer focus solely on traditional metrics like data volume or computing speed. Instead, we should look for companies that are driving organizational culture and process change. True value lies not in single-point technological breakthroughs but in platforms that can coordinate entire distributed data ecosystems. Pay attention to companies with clear visions committed to building an open, interconnected network of data products. They are the ultimate winners of this paradigm shift.

The future of data is distributed. The task of building this future has begun. Now, join us in pioneering the next chapter of data infrastructure.
