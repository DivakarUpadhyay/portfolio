# Embedded Finance: Why Every Company Will Become a Fintech Company

A highly impactful fact is revealing the future landscape of the digital economy: according to in-depth research by Bain & Company, embedded finance alone accounted for nearly 5% of total U.S. financial transactions in 2021, corresponding to transaction volumes as high as $2.6 trillion. And this is just the beginning—the report predicts that by 2026, this figure will surge to over $7 trillion, accounting for more than 10% of total transaction value [1]. This is not marginal growth but a massive structural shift. If financial services are the "electricity of the digital world," then we are witnessing them evolve from a scarce resource that needed to be specifically sought out, to infrastructure that is ubiquitous and available on-demand, just like electricity. Against this backdrop, a counterintuitive argument emerges: the future of finance may well be the "disappearance" of finance—it will be deeply integrated into every application we use, becoming invisible, imperceptible, yet omnipresent.

### Paradigm Shift: From Destination to Infrastructure

For a long time, financial services have followed a "destination" model. Whether as individuals or businesses, when we need loans, insurance, or payment services, we must proactively go to a specific "destination"—a bank's physical branch, official website, or an insurance company's office. The entire industry's operating logic has revolved around how to attract customers to these channels controlled by financial institutions. The core assumption of this model is that financial services are independent, separately sellable products, and customers are rational seekers who will cross channel and platform boundaries to obtain services.

However, this institution-centric traditional model is being disrupted by an entirely new and more powerful logic. We are experiencing a fundamental paradigm shift from "banking as destination" to "Banking as a Service" (BaaS). In this new world, finance is no longer viewed as an independent "product" but is deconstructed into a series of flexibly callable "capabilities." Core financial functions such as payments, credit, insurance, and identity verification are packaged as standardized APIs (Application Programming Interfaces), allowing any non-financial company to easily integrate these capabilities into their own products and services. This means that the core of value creation is quietly shifting from traditional financial institutions to platforms that truly control customer relationships and application scenarios. This is not just a channel revolution but a profound reshaping of business models, with implications as far-reaching as cloud computing's complete transformation of the software industry.

### Why Now: The Resonance of Three Catalysts

The arrival of this revolution is no accident but the result of three key forces—technology, market, and data—achieving perfect resonance at the current point in time. Together, they are dismantling the high walls of traditional financial services, allowing innovation to pour in.

**First is the technology catalyst: the maturation of the API economy and BaaS stack.** If APIs are the universal language that enables different software services to "communicate" with each other, then "Banking as a Service" (BaaS) is the operating system built on this language. A typical BaaS technology stack usually contains three layers. At the bottom are licensed, strictly regulated banks—they are the ultimate providers of financial products and bearers of risk. The middle layer consists of the key enablers of this revolution, companies like **Stripe**, **Marqeta**, and **Plaid**. They play the role of "financial translators," converting the complex, outdated systems of underlying banks into modern, developer-friendly API interfaces, handling a large number of technical and compliance challenges. The top layer consists of platforms that directly face end customers—they use the APIs provided by the middle layer to seamlessly integrate financial functions into their own applications. The proliferation of cloud computing and standardization of technology has made this "plug-and-play" model extremely low-cost, dramatically lowering the barrier for non-financial enterprises to offer financial services—from years of time and tens of millions of dollars in investment, down to weeks and minimal startup costs [2].

**Second is the market catalyst: the expectations of digital natives are reshaping the B2B space.** We have long been accustomed to enjoying seamless digital experiences in the consumer (B2C) space, but this force is penetrating the enterprise (B2B) world at an unprecedented pace. The new generation of enterprise decision-makers and employees are themselves digital natives who can no longer tolerate the friction-filled manual processes prevalent in B2B procurement, payment, and financing. They expect to have the same smooth experience at work as using consumer-grade apps. The rise of the platform economy has not only educated the consumer market but has also set benchmarks for the B2B space, demonstrating the tremendous value that one-stop integrated experiences can create. When business owners are already accustomed to opening stores and getting loans with one click on Shopify, they naturally expect the same convenience in their industry software. This shift in market expectations provides strong pull for embedded finance innovation in the B2B space.

**Finally and most critically is the data catalyst: the power of contextual data.** Traditional financial institutions heavily rely on standardized, often lagging financial statements when conducting risk assessments. But embedded finance participants—the platforms—sit on a data goldmine: real-time, dynamic, and highly relevant "contextual data." For example, restaurant POS system **Toast** understands a restaurant's daily customer traffic, table turnover rate, and cash flow far better than any bank; construction project management software **Procore**'s grasp of a project's progress, material procurement, and subcontractor payments is unmatched by traditional credit institutions. This deep data insight forms a powerful "data flywheel": platforms use scenarios to generate exclusive data, use data for more precise risk assessment and product design, thereby providing financial products that are better and more convenient than those of traditional financial institutions, which in turn attracts more users to the platform, generating even more data. This is a self-reinforcing positive cycle that constitutes the deepest moat of embedded finance. Contextual data is not just another data source—it fundamentally changes the rules of the game for risk pricing and product innovation.

### Deconstructing the New Value Chain

The rise of embedded finance has given birth to an entirely new ecosystem and value chain. Bain & Company's research clearly outlines four core participants in this ecosystem:

1.  **End Customer**: The ultimate users of financial services, which can be individual consumers or businesses.
2.  **Platform**: The primary interface interacting with end customers, controlling customer relationships and application scenarios. For example, e-commerce platform Shopify or health management app Mindbody.
3.  **Enabler**: BaaS providers that offer critical API technology and infrastructure to help platforms integrate financial services.
4.  **License Provider**: Typically regulated banks or financial institutions that provide compliant financial products and license support.

To more clearly understand this transformation, we can compare the fundamental differences between traditional finance and embedded finance value chains through the table below:

| **Element** | **Traditional Finance Value Chain** | **Embedded Finance Value Chain** |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer Acquisition** | Banks acquire customers directly through advertising, branches, marketing campaigns | Platforms trigger financial needs in specific scenarios among their existing customer base |
| **Product Distribution** | Distribution through banks' own closed channels | Open distribution on any third-party platform through enabler APIs |
| **Risk Assessment** | Primarily relies on standardized historical financial data and credit scores | Primarily relies on real-time, dynamic contextual data provided by platforms |
| **Customer Relationship** | Directly owned and managed by banks | Primarily owned and managed by platforms, banks retreat to the background |

### Future Vision: Deepening in B2C and Explosion in B2B

The future of embedded finance will continue to deepen in the B2C space and experience true explosion in the B2B space.

In the **B2C space**, innovation will go far beyond payments and simple "Buy Now, Pay Later" (BNPL). We will see the proliferation of **embedded insurance**—for example, when booking a flight on an airline website, the system will automatically recommend the most suitable travel inconvenience insurance based on your destination and itinerary; when purchasing cat food on a pet e-commerce platform, the platform will offer you a customized pet health insurance plan. **Embedded investing** will also become within reach—for example, in a popular budgeting or savings app, you can set up automatic investment of spare change from each purchase or weekly surplus into a low-cost index fund. Even **embedded health** will combine your wearable device data to provide dynamically priced personalized health insurance.

However, the greater opportunity lies in the **B2B space**, which is a vast new continent with enormous potential. According to Bain's analysis, in B2B payments alone, transaction volumes processed through embedded finance are expected to reach an astonishing $16 trillion [3]. Innovation here will focus on solving core efficiency problems in business operations. Imagine **embedded B2B payments** deeply integrating financing and payment processes into large enterprises' ERP or procurement software, fundamentally solving the chronic problems of information asymmetry and cumbersome processes in supply chain finance. **Embedded expense management** will become widespread—SaaS companies can issue smart corporate cards deeply integrated with their software functions, making expense reporting, approval, and financial reconciliation processes completely automated. **Embedded business lending** will become more prevalent—B2B marketplace platforms can provide instant, collateral-free working capital loans based on sellers' historical sales data and inventory on their platforms, helping small and medium enterprises solve urgent cash flow needs.

### Endgame: The Victory of Vertical Integration

We firmly believe that the ultimate battleground of embedded finance is not horizontal platforms offering general financial products, but "Vertical SaaS" companies that deeply understand the pain points and workflows of specific industries. These companies are evolving from pure software providers to become the business operating systems of their industries, with embedded finance being their sharpest weapon for building moats.

Let's look at a few examples. In the **restaurant industry**, **Toast** started by providing cloud-based POS systems, but today it also offers restaurants a range of financial services including working capital loans, employee payroll management, and vendor payments. These services are valuable precisely because they are deeply tied to restaurants' core business activities—collecting payments, paying employees, and procurement. In **construction**, project management platforms like **Procore** could easily embed services such as project financing, installment payments for building materials, and real-time payroll for workers, addressing long-standing capital turnover challenges in the industry. In the more traditional **agriculture sector**, we can also imagine agtech platforms using IoT sensors and satellite imagery data to provide farmers with more precise crop insurance linked to weather and soil conditions, or financing lease solutions based on farm equipment usage data.

Vertical integration is the endgame because it creates the strongest network effects and highest switching costs. When an enterprise's core business flow and financial flow both deeply depend on one platform, it becomes nearly impossible to switch to other competitors.

### A Call to Builders

This revolution led by embedded finance brings entirely new challenges and opportunities to every participant in the ecosystem.

**For entrepreneurs, our advice is: "Follow the Friction."** Rather than building a comprehensive digital bank from scratch, look for a vertical industry you deeply understand and find the areas where inefficiency is caused by the absence, misalignment, or high cost of traditional financial services. Solve those "unsexy" but highly valuable problems, such as complex accounts receivable reconciliation and factoring between B2B enterprises, or financing gaps in specific industry supply chains. These friction-filled places are where innovation value is greatest.

**For traditional financial institutions, it's time to complete a critical role transition: from "star" to "producer."** In the wave of embedded finance, clinging to front-end customer channels poses a huge risk of being "pipelined." Rather than passively watching value drain away, it's better to proactively transform and become powerful "license providers" and "enablers" in the new ecosystem. Export your core capabilities in compliance, risk control, and fund management at scale through BaaS services, becoming the "arsenal" behind this transformation. This is not only a defensive path but also an offensive path to scalable growth.

**For investors, we should look for the "new generation of system integrators."** Future winners will be those vertical platform companies that can most perfectly combine software, data, and financial services. Beyond continuing to watch the "pickaxe sellers" (B2B infrastructure providers), we should focus our attention on companies that are using these pickaxes to dig the deepest moats in specific industries. They are the greatest beneficiaries of this transformation.

### Conclusion

The future of finance is embedded, invisible, and context-aware. It is no longer a standalone industry but is becoming infrastructure that drives innovation and growth across all industries, like electricity and the internet. When every company can become a fintech company, the wave of innovation will not only transform the financial industry itself but will also bring unprecedented efficiency gains and value creation to every corner, from retail and restaurants to construction and agriculture. This is a quiet but far-reaching revolution, and builders are at its center.
